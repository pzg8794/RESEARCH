Dear Prof. Cherubin and Search Committee,

I am applying for the PhD Candidate position in Quantum Compiler Technologies at NTNU. The Smart Precision for Quantum Resilience (SPQR) project is precisely the research problem I want to work on at the doctoral level, and the System Software Group is the right setting to do it.

Prof. Cherubin's research trajectory — from compiler-assisted dynamic precision tuning to the TAFFO framework — defines the methodological core of what SPQR proposes to extend: taking precision-resource trade-off analysis developed for classical floating-point computation and adapting it to a domain where the stakes are logical-qubit program correctness. That translation challenge, from TAFFO's fixed-point optimization model to error-correction code selection for logical qubit implementations, is technically deep and scientifically novel. It demands formal rigor, implementation depth, and reproducible experimental methodology — exactly the research mode I am preparing for.

My technical background positions me to contribute at the implementation level from the start. My MS thesis in Computer Science was implemented in C and C#, building real-time recognition software requiring careful reasoning about computation-correctness trade-offs and precision bounds at the systems level. I have taught C++ across undergraduate, graduate, and professional contexts, and I am actively deepening LLVM/MLIR implementation background in parallel with this application. The same compiler infrastructure TAFFO was built on is where I intend to contribute.

My current research as a Graduate Assistant at RIT focuses on adaptive AI for fault-tolerant quantum network optimization — contextual bandit algorithms and hybrid variable frameworks for routing under threat and capacity constraints. This has required modeling error-sensitivity trade-offs, building reproducible experimental pipelines, and producing results toward peer-reviewed publication. The connection to SPQR is direct: both are structured optimization problems over correctness-resource trade-off surfaces, with the compiler IR now taking the role my routing model plays in the network context.

At NTNU, I plan to contribute on three concrete lines: formalizing precision-resource trade-off structures at the quantum compiler IR level, building on TAFFO's analytical approach; implementing analysis and transformation passes in C/C++ within LLVM/MLIR-compatible infrastructure; and building reproducible benchmarking protocols targeting CGO and quantum software venues. I am strongly motivated by the collaboration with Politecnico di Milano, where the foundational precision tuning work underpinning this project was originally developed.

NTNU's rigorous research culture and the concrete scope of SPQR make this an exceptional setting for doctoral work. I would be honored to contribute.

Sincerely,

Piter Garcia
